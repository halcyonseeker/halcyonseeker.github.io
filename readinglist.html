Reading List

Some cool stuff to read. Topics and genres range wildley. I've done my best to roughly categorize them below, but of course categorization along the lines of topic and genre is far from an exact science.

Nonfiction

The Opposite of Hate – Sally Kohn

This book is first in the list for a good reason.

In The Beginning there was the Command Line – Neal Stephenson

Neal Stephenson spends 60 pages waxing philosophical about GUIs, CLIs, and the Morloks and the Eloi while getting his geography and history wrong. Its a fascinating essay and you should read it. Also, a cool side note, in an analogy between operating systems and cars, he makes mention of geodesic domes in the hippy encampment of linux; the history and engineering of Buckminster Fuller's geodesic domes and their relationship with American counter-culture is really interesting.

The Art of Unix Programming – Erik S. Raymond

As far as the subject matter goes, the title is pretty self explanitory. I've been reading this book over the past few months; the parts that I can understand have been really interesting, and the parts that I don't yet understand have been rather boring. I've learned a lot and though it is rather old (published in 2003), I recommend it for anybody with an interest in Unix like operating systems. You can read it here.

The Boy Who Harnassed the Wind – William Kamkwamba

This is the biographic story of how a poor kid in rural Malawi taught himself about physics and engineering and electronics from books at his local library then built a windmill to bring electricity into his family home. I think he got a scholarship to a fancy European school and is currently working on bringing sustainable technology to rural Africa.

Science Fiction and Fantasy

The Grand Tour series – Ben Bova

Bova's Grand Tour is a voluminous sience fiction series that begins at an indeterminated time around the turn of the turn of the millenium and follows a diverse cast of characters centuries into the future, documenting humanity's expansion into the solar system. Becuase a lot of the events of the series happened in what is now the past, it seems pretty anachronistic at times, though personally I rather enjoy that feeling of displacement. My main gripe with what I've read of the series is that the science behind the constant, underlying theme of climate catastrophe in the books is rather unrealistically optimistic, though that is not big deal. This series is pretty disjointed, so you can read it in almost any order you wish, though there are several mini-series' within the Grand Tour that you should read in order. Personally, I prefer to read it mostly chronologicaly, as subsequent books tend to subtly build upon each other.

The Moon is a Harsh Mistress – Robert Heinlein

This book takes place in the 2080s and is basically about a computer engineer from Luna who narrates how he led a revolution that won independence from Terra. Unlike most revolutionary fiction, it focuses substantailly on the logistics and theory of revolution and governance, and, like the equally fantastic Starship Troopers, it spends a fair amount of time discussing political philosophy. This book also serves to showcase some interesting cultural adaptations that have occured on the moon. I find the concepts of polyandrous line marriges and tight community justice to be pretty intruiging.

Have Spacesuit, Will Travel – Robert Heinlein

A freakishly intelligent teenage protagonist teaches himself about science and electronics by fixing a spacesuit before going on a bizarre and surreal inter-galactic adventure. It's basically a less existential, hard science version of The Hitchhicker's guide to the galaxy. And yes, Heinlein is one of my favorite authors.

Starship Troopers – Robert Heinlein

Revelation Space – Alastair Reynolds

The Hitchhicker's Guide To the Galaxy – Douglas Adams

I love this book so much, where do I even start?

Seveneves – Neal Stephenson

Seveneves documents the reaction of the human race to an extinction event. Of all quality sci-fi I've read, Seveneves is one of the only ones that has profoundly effected me in some way. You should read it.

General Fiction

Cryptonomicon – Neal Stephenson

The Lords of Discipline – Pat Conroy

I'm really not sure how to describe this book. It's intense. Just read it.

Little Brother – Correy Doctrow

This book is great. It follows a teenage hacker in San Fransisco after a massive terrorist attack and follows his efforts to fight back against the DHS. This is one of those fiction books that is not quite fiction, there are some significant parallels between Little Brother's dystopian San Fransisco and the post-911 USA. Also, unlike most people who write hacker protagonists, the author really knows his shit and explains computer stuff in a really accesible fashion. It's published under a copyleft liscense so you can either buy a hard copy or download it for free here.

Ideas and Philosophy

Industrial Society and Its Future – Theodore Kaczynski

Uncle Ted: Ideas Worth Spreading

Meditations – Marcus Aurelius

The Sovereign (Prince) – Nicolo Machievelli

The Art of War – Sun Tzu

the GNU Manifesto – Richard Stallman

An interesting read on digital ethics. The GNU (GNU's Not Unix) manifesto outlines the motivations for the creation of the GNU system, and the ideological foundations of the free software movement.
