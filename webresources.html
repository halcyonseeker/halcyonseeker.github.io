Things of Interest to the Self-Taught Computer Freak

As of the creation of this document, it's been about two years since the beginning of my nose-dive into the realm of computing. Though the catalyst for my obsession was a high-school computer science course, the overwhelming majority of my computer knowledge has been self taught. Though I believe that the best way to teach yourself about something is to get your hands dirty and break things, theoretical and second-hand knowledge are also essential.

The purpose of this document is to serve as a central directory and hub for resources I've found useful, am acively using, and think would be useful but have not gotten around to exploiting yet. Also, it serves the alterior motive of cleaning out my voluminous and chaotic bookmarks.html file.

Topics of interest include, but are not limited to:

  • ThiccPads
  • Retrocomputing
  • Linux/BSD/Unix system administration (such a boring term for such a cool subject)
  • Amatuer radio, GMRS radio, packet radio, and decentralized communications in general
  • Weird shit like TempleOS and Roko's Baselisk
  • Techniques for the Preservation of Precious Bodily Fluids
  • (Spac)emacs and Org-Mode
  • Web design and related bigotries
  • Various programming languages (*nix shell, elisp, C, awk, assembly, python, etc)
  • Arduinos and stuff
  • Philosophies of computer use

General

Wikipedia. The usefulness of the Free Encyclopedia cannot be stressed enough. But remember kids, don't cite Wikipedia, it is best used as a launching off point into more reputable resources, and a source of things to read when you're bored.

Read The F*****g Manual. The most useful way of learning about something computer related is usually to read the manual or manuals. Links to manuals, both official and unofficial, that I've found useful are linked in the manual section at the bottom of this page.

The Absolute Basics

My high school computer science teacher's website. Everything is focused around Jabba the Hutt but the information is accessible and it covers most of the bases pretty well.

Beginning Git and Github for Linux Users. A helpful introduction to using Git on the command line.

Crash Course Computer Science. I found Crash Course's Computer Science series to be pretty helpful when first getting into computer science. Like all of Crash Course's content they start really basic and rapidly escalate, so for best effect you should probably watch this series several times.

Harvard's CS50 Lectures. Harvard publishes the lectures of its cs50 class on the web. For lectures, they're pretty excelent, starting at a really basic level and escaating at a pace that I found comfortable. Just a warning, if you are like me and you like to download stuff onto your computer, the lectures are uploaded to YouTube in 4k. I forgot to check that in advance, and it took me about 24 hours to download some 50 gigs of content. I think that there is a way to force youtube-dl to download it a lower resolution. RTFM.

Programming

ergoemacs.org. The guy who writes this blog has a gift for explaining seemingly complex stuff in really simple terms. I've found his elisp tutorials to be really helpful at getting down the basics.

Emergency Lisp. I've not really used it but it looks good.

Sorting Algorithms in C. A decent article on basic sorting algorithms in the C programming language.

Read Lisp, Tweak Emacs. An introduction to elisp with a specific focus on customizing Emacs.

Starting Forth. Forth is such a cool language, and this book is a really good way of getting into it. Fyi, I highly recommend reading this book with an open terminal running gforth (gnu-forth).

Java, Java, Java by Morelli & Walde. Jabba the Hutt is a bloated language, but this book is soo good.

*nix and Web Stuff

Mastering the Web: The ultimate guide for indie web developers. Really high quality, but often incomplete information on OpenBSD administration and back-to-the-basics web development.

c0ffee.net. More good stuff about BSD administration. This blog has an emphasis on self hosting and running BSD on

openBSD.org. The entire website is a gold mine of excelent documentation and faqs. Also, OpenBSD is famous for its well-written man pages.

The Linux Documentation Project. Pretty self explanitory.

The Arch Wiki. Regaurdless of what Linux distribution you use, the Arch Wiki is an invaluable resource for just about everything. Btw, I use Arch Linux.

Get on the D-BUS. An old article on interacting with D-BUS

Assembly and Low-Level Things

Down to the Silicon: How the Z80's Registers are implemented. A look at the architecture of the Z80 cpu. I barely understand any of it yet, but I some may find it helpful or interesting.

Learn Multi-Platform Z80 assembly programming. An overview of Z80 programming and a hub for related Z80 resources, all with some hideously retro css.

Cidco Mailstation as a Z80 Development Platform. An interesting blogpost about a self contained Z80 computer with networking and a full qwerty keyboard.

Tools for the above Cidco Mailstation.

Writing a boot loader in Assembly and C - Part 1. An interesting looking article in stilted English that I've been meaning to get to.

Writing a Bootloader. At a cursory glance this appears to be interesting and easy to follow, if somewhat light on the details.

osdev.org. A wiki devoted to the writing of operating systems.

Manuals

Btw, when using pdf manuals the Zathura document viewer with a pdf compatability extension makes things much more pleasent. Zathura is a really great, keyboard driven document viewer available for GNU/Linux and probably the BSDs as well.

The Not so Short Introduction to LaTeX. An extremely useful introduction to typesetting in the LaTeX markup language. Now that I've gotten decent with Org-mode (see below) I don't need to write stuff in LaTeX much anymore.

The Org Manual. This manual is one of the best written I've come across. It is really accesible and even enjoyable to read. It sounds stupid, but honestly I find reading it to be something of an adventure; it's really easy to get sucked down a rabbit hole of learning about org-mode's power.
