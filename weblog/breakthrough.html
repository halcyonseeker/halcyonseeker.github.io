Book Review: Breakthrough by Jack Andraka

The Book

Breakthrough follows the life of Jack Andraka from his early childhood, detailing the pressures and events that led him to invent an early detection test for pancreatic cancer. Overall this book is extremely accessible, it's written very well with a cohesive and easy to follow storyline. The author explains the scientific concepts in plain English, allowing readers who are not well versed in science to follow along with minimal difficulty. For me, the highlight of the story was the explanation of the scientific process that led Jack to make his discovery. As someone who is quite poorly versed in the fields of biology and medicine, the accessible writing style really helped.

The Science

In order to detect an illness you need to be able to detect its byproducts. If you do not know its byproducts, you will not be able to detect it. Using an article from the open-access Public Library of Science, Jack trawled through a database of 8,000 proteins found in various bodily fluids of Pancreatic Cancer patients. He examined each protein through a set of criteria:

  • Up-regulated or down-regulated: basically, whether or not the amount of proteins in the blood increase or decrease with disease. Because there would be more of them, it would be easier to detect up-regulated proteins.
  • If the protein was sensitive to diseases other than Pancreatic Cancer, it would not be a reliable indicator. Many of the proteins Jack examined were poorly documented, making research in this area quite difficult.

After a few months he was able to narrow down to a set of 50 proteins that either met the above criteria, or were insufficiently researched. He then ran each of those through another set of criteria, including what bodily fluids they are found in. Because one of the original goals of Jack's research was to find a way to detect pancreatic cancer that was both cheap and easy, the biomarker protein would need to be found in an easily accessible bodily-fluid like blood or urine. About half way through the list of 50, he found a protein that fit the bill: mesothelin.

Mesothelin is a protein found in very high levels in the blood of people in the earliest, and most treatable, stages of pancreatic, lung, or ovarian cancer. But finding a suitable protein was only half of what had to be done. Jack still had to come up with a cheap, easy way of detecting it. After researching various expensive and complex detection methods, he came up with a hypothetical solution.

Basically, he would combine carbon nanotubes with specific antibodies to create a network that only reacts to a specific protein. When the mesotheline reacts with the antibodies they would form an immunocomplex, basically a massive protein molecule. When this large molecule forms, it is large enough to push aside neighboring nanotubes, causing the network to become more spread-out. This would reduce the number of contacts between the carbon nanotubes, creating fewer pathways in which electrons to flow. This would increase the electrical resistance of the network, something that can be easily measured with an ohmmeter.

The final product consisted of a network of carbon nanotubes and mesothelin-sensitive antibodies layed over a firm backing. A drop of blood would be placed on it and if its electrical resistance increased, then the doctors would know that the patient has high levels of mesothelin in their blood, indicating the presence of lung, ovarian, or pancreatic cancer.

Implications

When he decided that he wanted to prevent deaths by pancreatic cancer, the first thing Jack Andraka did was go to the internet and learn about pancreatic cancer. If I search for pancreatic cancer in Google, the search engine he most likely used, I get over 53 million results. The greatest thing about the internet is that it places the knowledge of the world--sometimes literally--at your fingertips. I think it is safe to say that if Jack had not had access to the internet, he would never have gotten as far as he did.

Though the internet can give the average person access to vast sums of knowledge, most hard science is published in journals that are locked behind paywalls. This makes it extremely difficult for the average person--let alone a broke teenager--to gain access to actual scientific research. After learning all he could about pancreatic cancer on the public internet, Jack attempted to dive into the scientific literature, but often found his way forward hindered by paywalls. Fortunately, his parents were able to help him access some of the information he needed, but the presence of monolithic paywalls blocking access to scientific research could well have ended his endeavor.

Even when his parents capitulated and payed for an article, it was not uncommon for it to be so thick with jargon, that Jack, a teenager lacking in formal science education could not read it. Dense jargon is something that I have a personal dislike for. While jargon is often useful at conveying more complex ideas, it can impede those less formally educated, and its gratuitous over-use (likely for the purpose of making people sound smart) is just confusing.

Following a vast amount of research, Jack needed a laboratory. Out of 200 emails sent, only one person offered him access to a lab. Here again, his lack of formal training came back to bite him, almost ruining his experiment on multiple occasions. If he had not gained access to a lab with expensive equipment, he never would have been able to create a working prototype.

Following the media frenzy around his success in science fairs, the detection method Jack Andraka had pioneered moved on to real world testing. As of the writing of this, it is still under review by the FDA.
